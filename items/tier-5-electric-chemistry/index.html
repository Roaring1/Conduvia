    Field notes¶

    The real process behind these items — what they are, how they were used, and why they matter. Tap to expand.

    AFA-frame gin pole

    Temporary A-frame crane with a pulley for lifting heavy loads.

    A lashed A-frame with a pulley or block hung at the apex, guyed by ropes and used as a simple hand-powered crane for raising loads.

    Real-world use · Crews assemble a-frame gin poles from on-site timbers, lash them, rig a block and tackle, and then hoist stones, logs, or machinery where full cranes cannot reach or are not economical.

    • When · Ancient through modern construction; used in classical building, medieval works, and early industrial sites, and still used in remote or temporary jobs.
    • Where · Building sites, dockyards, logging camps, tower erection, and any place lifting capacity is needed without a permanent crane.

    Why it matters · An A-frame gin pole is the simplest crane you can build from local wood and rope. The frame carries compression, the guys manage tipping, and the pulley and rope translate human pull into vertical lift. In Conduvia it’s the entry point for believable heavy lifting before iron cranes and powered hoists arrive.
