    Field notes¶

    The real process behind these items — what they are, how they were used, and why they matter. Tap to expand.

    ADadze

    Perpendicular-bladed edge tool for hewing and smoothing timbers.

    A woodworking tool with its blade set crosswise to the handle, swung in a chopping motion to hew, hollow, and smooth beams and planks.

    Real-world use · Carpenters used adzes to flatten the faces of logs into square beams, shape ship timbers, and hollow troughs and bowls. The tool bridges rough felling work and finer planing, especially on long timbers where planes are awkward.

    • When · Stone and Bronze Age origins; common in ancient Egypt and the Mediterranean, through medieval carpentry and shipbuilding into the early 20th century, with specialty use continuing today.
    • Where · Found anywhere heavy timber was worked by hand: building sites, shipyards, bridge and trestle work, barn framing, and traditional carpentry shops.

    Why it matters · The adze is one of the oldest woodworking tools--essentially an axe turned sideways. By turning the blade perpendicular to the handle, workers can swing it in a controlled arc along the surface of a beam, shaving chips instead of simply chopping. It shines anywhere you need flat or gently hollow surfaces on large stock: ship’s ribs, sill timbers, ties, or troughs. In-game, it stands for “serious” timber shaping before sawmills and power planers exist.

    AGanimal glue

    Protein-based adhesive rendered from hides and bones.

    A hot-mixed glue made by boiling animal collagen, used for wood joints, veneering, and repairs that can later be reversed with heat and moisture.

    Real-world use · Luthiers, furniture makers, and joiners relied on animal glue for centuries. It grips well, creeps little under load, and joints can be steamed apart for repair or disassembly.

    • When · Documented in ancient Egypt and classical woodworking; dominant wood glue in Europe up through the 19th century and still used in conservation and fine instruments.
    • Where · Carpentry and joinery shops, cabinetmakers’ benches, and any place doing serious wood assembly before synthetic resins.

    Why it matters · Animal glue is how complex wooden artifacts stayed together long before bottled glue existed. It requires heat and timing but rewards good fits: the glue is strongest when the wood surfaces already match. In-game it supports a believable jump from purely mechanical joints and lashings to more compact, rigid constructions.

    BAblock-and-tackle

    Paired pulley blocks that multiply pulling force for lifting.

    Two or more pulleys (sheaves) rigged with rope so that pulling the free end lifts a load with mechanical advantage.

    Real-world use · Block-and-tackle systems hoist yards and sails, raise stones and timbers, tension rigging, and move machinery. They trade rope travel and complexity for greatly reduced effort per unit load.

    • When · Used in classical Greek engineering; widespread on sailing ships and construction sites through the age of sail and beyond.
    • Where · Ships, docks, construction scaffolding, mines, and any rigging-heavy environment.

    Why it matters · Block and tackle embodies “work in, work out”. You pull a lot of rope to move the load a little, but each pull is manageable. In Conduvia it explains why small crews can realistically raise massive beams, boiler shells, and stones without magical strength.

    BDbow drill

    Cord-wrapped spindle driven by a bow for drilling and friction fire.

    A straight spindle wrapped with a corded bow, guided by a hand socket or cap, used to spin quickly for small holes or to start fires by friction.

    Real-world use · Bow drills bored neat, small-diameter holes in wood, bone, and soft stone long before metal twist drills. The same setup, with a fireboard and tinder, was used to raise an ember for campfires and hearths.

    • When · Documented in ancient Egypt and the Near East; used across classical, medieval, and early modern periods wherever simple drilling or friction fire was needed.
    • Where · Common around early woodworking benches, bowyers’ shops, jewelry and bead workshops, and among travelers needing a compact fire-starting kit.

    Why it matters · The bow drill solves a basic problem: your hands can’t spin a stick fast enough for long enough. By tying the spindle to a bowstring, you trade awkward spinning for a smooth sawing motion of the bow. A socket stone on top keeps the spindle vertical and takes the thrust. In practice it’s fussy but powerful: good for delicate holes and a great way to explain how rotational speed and pressure combine to do work.

    BSbow saw frame

    Tensioned frame saw that keeps a thin blade tracking straight.

    A wooden frame that holds a narrow saw blade under tension, allowing straight or gently curved cuts through branches and planks.

    Real-world use · Frame saws cut firewood, planks, and joinery shoulders using replaceable blades. The bow keeps the blade tight so it won’t buckle in the kerf, and the handle angle can be shifted for ripping or crosscutting.

    • When · Used since at least Roman times; very common in medieval and early modern woodworking and still used in traditional carpentry.
    • Where · Found on building sites, in carpenters’ shops, and with woodcutters processing fuel or structural stock.

    Why it matters · A frame saw stretches a thin blade between two end pins and then pulls it tight with a cord and toggle or threaded stretcher. That lets you use much thinner steel than a rigid hand saw--less effort per cut and a narrower kerf--at the cost of some bulk from the frame. In Conduvia it represents a big jump in cutting efficiency over hacking timbers with axes alone.

    C(cam (wooden)

    Profiled wooden disk that turns rotation into timed lift.

    An off-center or oddly shaped wooden disk fixed to a shaft, used to periodically lift a follower like a tappet or hammer as it turns.

    Real-world use · Cams drive trip hammers, tappets for stamp mills, and valve linkages. Their profile controls timing and dwell, making them vital to repetitive mechanical cycles.

    • When · Documented in medieval water-driven mills and automata; central to many early industrial machines.
    • Where · Stamp mills, bellows drives, textile machinery, and any repetitive motion rig.

    Why it matters · The cam is one of the key steps from “continuous spin” to “discrete events”. By carving the cam face, millwrights encode timing into the shape of wood. In Conduvia, cams plus tappets justify automated stamping, pumping, and bellows operation once a steady shaft of power is available.

    CAcapstan

    Vertical drum turned by bars or belting to haul heavy lines.

    A vertical-axis cylinder around which rope or cable is wound; crew push on radial bars or a drive belt to turn it and haul the loaded line.

    Real-world use · Capstans raise anchors, haul ships along quays, tension rigging, and lift heavy cargo and stones. Friction between rope and drum allows slips and controlled holding.

    • When · Ancient uses on ships and siege works; standard gear on sailing ships and dockyards into the modern era.
    • Where · Ships, harbors, building hoists, and any site needing controlled heavy hauling.

    Why it matters · A capstan is a winch turned upright. It lets many people push in a circle, converting their effort into smooth, controllable haul on a line. In Conduvia it ties together rope, blocks, and fixed anchors into a reusable station for serious lifting and pulling jobs.

    CBcarving bench

    Low bench with stops and fixtures for carving and shaping work.

    A stout bench or plank rigged with dogs, stops, and sometimes a shaving horse-like clamp, used to hold small workpieces securely for carving and shaping.

    Real-world use · Specialized benches let workers carve handles, spokes, and decorative elements while seated or braced, keeping both hands free for tools.

    • When · Varied forms across pre-industrial woodworking, from medieval benches to 19th-century patternmakers’ setups.
    • Where · Woodshops, carvers’ corners in larger shops, and small cottage workshops producing shaped wooden components.

    Why it matters · Workholding is as important as the cutting tools themselves. A carving bench demonstrates how simple stops and clamps multiply precision and safety, letting makers take more aggressive cuts with control. In-game it can gate finer shaped parts and decorative work that would be unrealistic from a generic floor workspace.

    CHchisel

    Straight-edged tool for carving, joinery, and dressing stone.

    A bar of steel or stone with a sharpened edge at one end, struck with a mallet or pushed by hand to cut and pare material.

    Real-world use · Chisels form mortises, tenons, dovetails, and rebates in wood; they also dress stone, cut keyways, and clean up edges that saws and axes cannot reach cleanly.

    • When · Present from the Bronze Age onward in both wood and stone trades, with refined forms in classical, medieval, and industrial workshops.
    • Where · Universal tool across carpentry, masonry, metalworking, and sculptural shops.

    Why it matters · Chisels are the gateway between “rough shaping” and “precision”. They translate the blow of a mallet into a controlled slice, letting you sneak up on a line instead of smashing past it. The same idea works whether the cutting edge is hardened steel or carefully knapped stone. In-game, they justify all the neat sockets, grooves, and joinery that simple axes cannot produce alone.

    CTclay tuyere

    Thick ceramic nozzle that carries blast from bellows into a furnace while keeping the bellows themselves away from the fire.

    Real-world use · A tapered clay tube is mortared into the furnace wall. Bellows or blowpipes couple to the cool end; the hot end faces the burning fuel bed. The ceramic body resists heat better than metal in early furnaces and protects leather bellows from radiant heat.

    • When · Attested at prehistoric and early historic iron-smelting sites and small furnaces across Eurasia and Africa.
    • Where · Built into bloomery furnaces, small forges, and primitive cupolas wherever leather or wooden bellows need protection from direct flame.

    Why it matters · Without a tuyere, the bellows die first. The tuyere is therefore a quiet but essential invention: a replaceable, high-heat “fuse” between fragile bellows and the furnace interior. It also focuses the blast into the fuel bed, shaping the hottest zone. Understanding where the tuyere sits is key to understanding why some parts of a furnace case erode and why bloom or slag forms where it does.

    CScoarse screen

    Timber frame with coarse mesh that pre-screens feed material.

    A sturdy screen or grate with large openings, used to separate oversized rocks and debris from ore or aggregate before finer processing.

    Real-world use · Miners and millers screen excavated material to remove cobbles and trash, protecting crushers, sluices, and sieves from clogging and damage.

    • When · Simple screening with woven or bar grids is ancient; standardized screens appear in early industrial ore dressing.
    • Where · Mine heads, crushing floors, wash plants, and brick or lime works handling graded aggregate.

    Why it matters · Screening is one of the cheapest process improvements you can make. By removing junk and oversize pieces before delicate steps, you boost throughput and protect equipment. In Conduvia it represents sensible pre-processing before sluices, sieves, and mills touch valuable feed.

    COcounterweight

    Mass used to balance arms or store potential energy.

    A dense weight hung or fixed on a lever or drum to balance loads, smooth motion, or store energy that can be released later.

    Real-world use · Counterweights balance cranes and drawbridges, tension belts, regulate clockwork, and power treadle-like devices by descending under gravity.

    • When · Used in classical siege engines and water-raising devices; formalized in medieval and early modern clockwork and lifting machinery.
    • Where · Cranes, hoists, mill governor systems, clock towers, and lift mechanisms.

    Why it matters · Counterweights turn gravity into a patient, predictable helper. They can offset the weight of an arm so it feels neutral, or slowly release energy to drive a mechanism. In Conduvia they support more self-regulating and “coasting” machinery once players move beyond purely direct human power.

    DRdrawknife

    Two-hand pull blade for debarking and shaping long wooden pieces.

    A straight blade with handles at each end, pulled toward the worker to quickly peel bark and shave wood along the grain.

    Real-world use · Drawknives were used to debark logs, taper tool handles, shape chair parts, and rough in wheel spokes and staves. A shaving horse or bench holds the work while the worker leans back and pulls.

    • When · Well established by the late medieval period and common in rural and craft woodworking through the 19th century.
    • Where · Wheelwrights’ and coopers’ shops, chairmakers’ sheds, timber yards, and any place shaping long curved wood pieces.

    Why it matters · Unlike a plane, the drawknife has no sole--it’s all exposed blade and feedback. That makes it fast and versatile: you can take huge bites on green wood or whisper-thin shavings on seasoned stock just by changing your wrist angle and bite. In Conduvia it stands in for “fast, dirty shaping” that turns rough poles into finished parts before fine tools touch them.

    FLflywheel

    Heavy wheel that stores rotational energy and smooths power.

    A massive rotating wheel that resists speed changes, evening out pulses from hand or water power and carrying machinery through dead spots.

    Real-world use · Flywheels smooth reciprocating pumps and presses, keep line shafts from stalling under intermittent loads, and stabilize engine speed.

    • When · Implicit in early mill designs; mathematically analyzed and standardized in the 18th–19th centuries with steam engines.
    • Where · Stamp mills, presses, threshers, pumps, and any cycle-based machinery with uneven torque.

    Why it matters · A flywheel is a momentum battery. It doesn’t add power on its own, but by soaking up excess energy during “easy” parts of the cycle and giving it back during “hard” parts, it makes everything feel smoother and safer. In Conduvia it’s the bridge between choppy treadle/hand power and continuous rotary drives suitable for whole workshops.

    GPgold pan

    Shallow pan for washing sediments and concentrating heavy minerals.

    A shallow, often ridged pan swirled with water so light sand and gravel wash out, leaving dense particles like gold behind.

    Real-world use · Prospectors test stream gravels by panning small shovelfuls. Gentle swirling suspends and washes away lighter material while heavy grains and nuggets settle in the bottom or against riffles.

    • When · Used since ancient placer mining; iconic in 19th-century gold rushes and still used today for sampling and hobby panning.
    • Where · Any placer stream, test pit, or wash site where workers need a quick check on whether the gravels carry pay.

    Why it matters · The gold pan is a pocket-sized gravity concentrator. It demonstrates that you can separate materials just by exploiting density and water flow, long before complex machinery shows up. In-game, it justifies early “test for ore here” gameplay before players build rocker boxes and sluices for throughput.

    G(grease (tallow)

    Rendered animal fat used as a general-purpose lubricant.

    A semi-solid grease made from rendered animal fat, used to lubricate bearings, slides, and wooden parts exposed to friction.

    Real-world use · Tallow in bearings reduces wear and noise, protects metal from rust, and keeps wooden bearing surfaces from burning or glazing. It also appears in candles and waterproofing mixes with wax and pitch.

    • When · Ubiquitous wherever animals are processed; used from antiquity through the industrial age until mineral greases became cheap.
    • Where · Mills, wagons, windlasses, capstans, and any machinery with plain bearings or sliding wood-on-metal surfaces.

    Why it matters · Early machines don’t work for long without lubrication. Tallow is messy but easy to produce and reapply, and it turns shrieking wooden bearings into quiet, survivable interfaces. In Conduvia it underpins realistic maintenance loops: machines run better and last longer when players keep them greased.

    G(grog (crushed ceramic temper)

    Crushed, pre-fired ceramic added back into clay as temper to control shrinkage and cracking.

    Real-world use · Potters fire sacrificial clay, crush it, and sieve it into sharp, gritty grains. Mixed into fresh clay, grog opens the body, reduces plastic shrinkage, and helps pots and bricks survive drying and firing cycles.

    • When · Well attested archaeologically from ancient pottery traditions onward.
    • Where · Potteries and brickworks that re-use broken ware and kiln furniture as an ingredient rather than waste.

    Why it matters · Grog looks like rubble but acts like engineering. Because it has already been through the kiln, it does not shrink again; instead it acts as a skeletal framework inside the clay body. In practice this allows thicker pieces--such as crucibles, tuyere blocks, and big kiln bricks--to survive firing and thermal shock. It is one of the key “hidden” tricks that make reliable high-heat ceramics possible with modest technology.

    HOhoe

    L-shaped digging blade for cutting turf and shaping soil.

    A flat blade set at a right angle to a long handle, used to chop, pull, and scrape soil for beds, furrows, and weeding.

    Real-world use · Hoes open planting furrows, break crusted soil, hill crops, and slice weeds at the root. They are among the earliest farming tools and remain standard hand implements worldwide.

    • When · Neolithic agriculture onward; common in every period and region that farms by hand.
    • Where · Fields, kitchen gardens, canal and terrace work, and any settlement doing manual agriculture.

    Why it matters · The hoe shows how crude leverage plus a sharpened edge can reorganize whole landscapes. By pulling soil toward you instead of lifting with a shovel, it lets people manage wide beds with less strain. In-game it supports early farming and earthworks: beds, irrigation ditches, and rough grading before advanced implements appear.

    HOholdfast

    Bench clamp that wedges in a dog hole when struck and releases when tapped.

    A bent iron or wooden clamp dropped into a bench hole; a hammer blow wedges its foot onto the work, and a knock on the back frees it.

    Real-world use · Holdfasts pin boards and parts to a bench top for planing, chiseling, and carving. They are quick to set, adjustable, and require no screws or fine hardware.

    • When · Documented in medieval European bench designs and common in traditional woodworking into the present.
    • Where · Proper woodworking benches, carving benches, and any shop doing serious hand-tool work.

    Why it matters · The holdfast is a beautifully simple friction clamp. Instead of turning screws, you whack it to clamp and tap it to release, which keeps the work cycle fast. In Conduvia it supports stronger workholding so harsher tools--adzes, drawknives, chisels--can be used without the piece skating away.

    KVkiln vent baffle

    Movable plate or damper that throttles the kiln’s exhaust to control draft, temperature, and atmosphere.

    Real-world use · Potters and firemen adjust dampers at flues or chimney tops to slow or speed gas flow. Restricting the exit holds heat longer and can enrich reduction atmospheres; opening up cools and clears the kiln.

    • When · Appears with more permanent kilns; in use from antiquity through modern gas and wood kilns.
    • Where · Built into kiln stacks, flues, and chimney mouths wherever controlled firing is important.

    Why it matters · The baffle or damper is the kiln’s “throttle.” It does not create heat, but it governs how long hot gases stay in contact with the ware and how much fresh oxygen enters. In gameplay terms it justifies recipes that care about firing schedule or atmosphere. A world that can adjust kiln vents is starting to think in terms of process control rather than simply “add more fuel.”

    L(lever (pry bar)

    Stout bar used as a lever to nudge and lift heavy pieces.

    A rigid bar used under or against loads, with a fulcrum, so a modest input force can raise or shift heavy objects.

    Real-world use · Pry bars lift stone blocks just enough to shim, move timbers into line, and break loose stuck machinery. They are fundamental on any heavy work site.

    • When · As old as basic mechanics; used continuously across historical construction.
    • Where · Quarries, building sites, shipyards, rail and mill maintenance, and any place heavy pieces must be moved by hand.

    Why it matters · The lever is the simplest mechanical advantage device. A pry bar plus a rock under it lets a small crew “walk” huge blocks or frames into place a few centimeters at a time. In Conduvia it explains how players can realistically align heavy machinery and stonework even before powered jacks exist.

    LPlever press

    Compound lever that multiplies hand force into squeeze at a platen.

    A press where a long lever or series of levers drives a platen down on a workpiece, used for oil extraction, compaction, and forming.

    Real-world use · Lever presses squeeze oil from seeds, compact briquettes, and press simple shapes into soft metals or leather without need for screws or hydraulics.

    • When · Levers and presses appear in classical descriptions; vernacular presses remained common through the pre-industrial era.
    • Where · Oil mills, cider houses, small metal and leather shops, and experimental workshops.

    Why it matters · The lever press is a clean example of force multiplication through lever arms. It’s less precise than screw presses but easier to build from timber and a few iron straps. In Conduvia it enables pressing recipes--oils, cakes, bricks--before screw threads and hydraulics enter the tech tree.

    MAmallet

    Blocky wooden hammer that moves work without mauling the steel.

    A hammer with a large wooden head used to drive chisels, set joints, and knock assemblies apart without damaging tools or workpieces.

    Real-world use · Mallets strike chisels, adjust planes, seat joinery, and tap wedges. The broad wooden face spreads force and avoids marring handles and struck tools.

    • When · Used wherever woodworking is done; forms vary from simple clubs to carefully balanced joiner’s mallets.
    • Where · Carpenters’ benches, framing yards, shipyards, and any workshop driving chisels or assembling wooden structures.

    Why it matters · A mallet is basically a “forgiving” hammer. Because the head is wood, it dents instead of your tool handles or the workpiece. It lets carpenters really lean into chiseling and assembly without constantly smashing their gear. In Conduvia it pairs naturally with chisels, wedges, and holdfasts to justify more precise woodcraft.

    MCmeasuring cord

    Marked cord for measuring distances, layouts, and level lines.

    A length of cord with knots or marks at regular intervals, used to measure spans, set out rectangles, and transfer dimensions around a site.

    Real-world use · Builders used cords to lay out foundations, check diagonals for square, and mark repeated module lengths. Variation of tension and humidity made them imperfect, but they were cheap and quick compared to rods or chains.

    • When · Ancient builders’ tool; documented in Egyptian, Mesopotamian, and classical works; still used in masonry and carpentry.
    • Where · On building sites, field plots, ropewalks, and anywhere repeated measures are needed over tens of meters.

    Why it matters · A measuring cord turns “paces and guesses” into repeatable geometry. By marking off equal segments and checking diagonals, crews notice when a supposed rectangle is actually a rhombus. In Conduvia it explains how players can lay out straight roads, square foundations, and repeating bays long before steel tapes exist.

    PBplain bearing (wood)

    Shaped wooden block that supports a shaft directly, with grease.

    A wooden block or shell bored to fit a shaft, carrying it on a plain surface lubricated with grease instead of rolling elements.

    Real-world use · Wooden bearings carried waterwheel shafts, windmill spindles, and early line shafts. Properly greased and aligned they can support surprising loads, but they need monitoring and occasional replacement.

    • When · Common in pre-industrial mills and early factories before cheap metal bearings; still used in some low-speed, wet, or abrasive settings.
    • Where · Mill housings, waterwheel gudgeons, capstans, windlass drums, and early transmission shafts.

    Why it matters · A plain bearing is just a controlled sliding surface. Using wood as the bearing material keeps costs low and makes replacement easy, at the cost of higher friction and wear. In-game, these bearings let you build believable early power trains that demand lubrication and periodic rebuilds instead of running forever.

    PBplumb bob

    Weighted line that marks true vertical under gravity.

    A pointed weight hung from a string, used to find vertical lines and transfer points downward with high accuracy.

    Real-world use · Plumb bobs align walls, posts, and shafts; they help masons and carpenters keep structures truly upright and transfer alignment from stories above to foundations below.

    • When · Used since ancient Egypt and Mesopotamia and still standard in masonry and framing.
    • Where · Building sites, well- and shaft-sinking operations, shipyards aligning masts, and any work requiring true vertical.

    Why it matters · The plumb bob is a perfect example of exploiting physics rather than fighting it. Gravity is extremely consistent, so suspending a weight on a string gives you a reliable reference line anywhere. In-game it justifies how early crews can build surprisingly straight towers, shafts, and machinery without metal levels or lasers.

    PMpost mill (windmill)

    Early European windmill where the whole body turns around a central post.

    A windmill whose entire superstructure (“buck”) sits on and turns about a single vertical post so the sails can be yawed into the wind, often turned manually using a tailpole.

    Real-world use · Post mills drove millstones, pumps, and simple machinery wherever steady winds and modest mechanical power were available. They often formed the nucleus of rural processing--grinding grain, sawing wood, or pumping water.

    • When · Developed in medieval Europe by the 12th century; common through the 18th–19th centuries until superseded by smock and tower mills and later steam power.
    • Where · Windy ridges and open plains across northern Europe and other temperate regions, usually near grain-growing districts or drainage works.

    Why it matters · The post mill is the first major step from “one tool per worker” to “one engine drives many tools”. By putting the entire millhouse on a central post, millers can yaw it to face changing winds, then take off shaft power for stones, pumps, or light machinery below. In Conduvia it’s the iconic Tier-1 wind power station that rewards investment in sail cloth, carpentry, and careful siting on exposed ground.

    PUpulley

    Grooved wheel that redirects rope and forms the heart of lifting systems.

    A wheel with a grooved rim (sheave) that guides rope or cable, used singly to change direction or in sets to gain mechanical advantage.

    Real-world use · Single pulleys change pull direction to more convenient angles; multiple pulleys in blocks create hoisting systems and tensioners.

    • When · Known in classical Greek and earlier engineering; universal in rigging and machinery.
    • Where · Ships, cranes, hoists, mills, and any arrangement moving loads by rope.

    Why it matters · Pulleys don’t just make lifting easier--they make it possible to route force around corners. They are the building blocks for block-and-tackle, capstan leads, and belt idlers. In Conduvia they justify tidy rigging between distant drums, shafts, and loads without wild rope angles everywhere.

    RIriffle insert (sluice)

    Removable riffle tray that traps heavy particles in a sluice.

    A fitted insert carrying bars or cleats that create turbulence and low-speed eddies, capturing heavy minerals while lighter material washes over.

    Real-world use · Riffle inserts allow sluices to be cleaned quickly by lifting out the tray and washing captured concentrates into a pan, rather than dismantling the whole sluice.

    • When · Common in 19th-century placer mining and later alluvial operations; variations exist in earlier washing channels.
    • Where · Placer goldfields, tin and tungsten washing plants, and any gravity separation sluice.

    Why it matters · Riffles are the “teeth” of a sluice. They slow water just enough that dense grains settle and get caught, while trash and lighter sands keep moving. Making the riffles removable turns an all-day teardown into a quick cleanup cycle--perfect for gameplay loops around periodic concentrate collection.

    RBrocker box

    Portable cradle that washes pay dirt by rocking and sluicing.

    A box with riffles and a perforated upper tray, rocked by hand so water and gravel flow across, concentrating heavy minerals in the riffles.

    Real-world use · Rocker boxes were staples of early gold rushes: they allow miners to process more gravel than panning alone, with less water than full sluices.

    • When · Notable in 18th–19th century placer mining, especially during North American and Australian gold rushes.
    • Where · Temporary camp sites along creeks and bars where water supply is limited or ground must be sampled and moved often.

    Why it matters · The rocker box is a halfway step between pan and full sluice. By adding gentle, repeated motion, it automates part of the panning rhythm while staying portable. In Conduvia it’s an ideal mid-tier upgrade that rewards players who can supply steady water and muscle power.

    RWrope-walk jig

    Compact rig that twists fibers into rope along a guided walk.

    A line of hooks or spindles and a tailstock that twist multiple yarns together as they are walked out, forming rope under controlled tension.

    Real-world use · Full-scale ropewalks stretch for tens or hundreds of meters; a compact jig version allows small shops to twist cordage and moderate-length rope indoors or in tight yards.

    • When · Ropewalks are documented by the early modern period in Europe; smaller rigs echo the same geometry.
    • Where · Harbors, shipyards, industrial districts, and any settlement maintaining its own rigging and hoisting gear.

    Why it matters · Rope walks are early “factories” dedicated to a single product: line. The jig version in Conduvia abstracts that into a manageable footprint while keeping the core idea--multiple strands twisted together under tension. Using it in-game justifies higher-grade rope and specialty lines for hoists, sails, and belts.

    SCsail cloth

    Heavy canvas woven to catch wind on mills and boats.

    Durable, tightly woven cloth that forms sails for windmills and boats, converting wind pressure into useful force.

    Real-world use · Sail cloth covers mill sails, ship rigs, awnings, and tarps. It must balance weight, strength, and resistance to rot and tearing.

    • When · Linen and later cotton sail cloth are central from ancient seafaring civilizations through the age of sail, and on traditional windmills.
    • Where · Sail lofts, mills, harbors, and any industry leaning on wind power and weather protection.

    Why it matters · Sail cloth is what makes wind a usable “fuel”. Without good fabric, spars and frames do nothing. In Conduvia it is the gating material for both post mills and small craft, tying textile production directly into power infrastructure.

    SJscrew jack (wooden)

    Large wooden screw that raises loads slowly but with great force.

    A jack built around a coarse wooden screw and nut; turning the screw lifts or lowers a cap or pad that carries heavy loads.

    Real-world use · Wooden screw jacks align heavy stones, lift frames for shimming, and press loads where fine control and self-locking are more important than speed.

    • When · Historically tied to the rise of large wood screws in presses and lifting gear from the medieval period onward.
    • Where · Stone yards, bridge and building work, heavy woodworking shops, and early maintenance pits.

    Why it matters · The screw jack is a lever wrapped in a spiral. It trades rotation for lift so aggressively that a single worker can adjust tons of load a few millimeters at a time. In Conduvia it supports careful alignment of large machinery, kiln shells, and structural members beyond what pry bars and wedges alone can do.

    SHsheave

    The pulley wheel itself, fitted into blocks or frames.

    A grooved wheel that fits inside a block or frame, guiding rope or belt while minimizing friction.

    Real-world use · Sheaves are replaceable wear parts inside pulleys and blocks; different diameters and groove profiles match various lines and loads.

    • When · As old as pulley blocks themselves; wooden and later metal sheaves appear across historic rigging.
    • Where · Ship rigging, hoists, cranes, mills, and any mechanism using blocks-and-tackle or belt routing.

    Why it matters · Splitting “pulley” into sheave plus frame mirrors real millwright thinking. The sheave wears out first and is tuned to the rope; the frame attaches to the rest of the structure. In Conduvia, crafting sheaves separately allows more nuanced upgrades and repairs to rigging systems.

    SIsieve

    Fine mesh frame to separate small particles by size.

    A frame stretched with fine mesh used to separate meal from bran, fines from sand, or graded ore sizes by shaking or rubbing.

    Real-world use · Sieves refine flour, classify crushed ore, and sort sands and aggregates into useful fractions for casting or construction.

    • When · Used domestically and industrially since antiquity; materials range from woven plant fibers to metal wire cloth.
    • Where · Kitchens, mills, assay works, foundries, and building yards.

    Why it matters · Size classification is critical in both food and ore handling. Sieves allow different process streams--fine meal for baking, coarser for mash; fine ore for leaching, coarse for re-crushing. In Conduvia they make “grading” a visible, physical step rather than a magical sort.

    SBsluice box

    Inclined trough with riffles that washes pay dirt continuously.

    A long trough set on a gentle slope, lined with riffles or mats, where water carries sediment while heavy particles settle and lodge behind the riffles.

    Real-world use · Sluices process far more material than pans or rocker boxes, turning a steady water flow and shoveling into continuous concentration of heavy minerals.

    • When · Used in placer mining from at least the 18th century, with refinements through the 19th and 20th centuries.
    • Where · Permanent or semi-permanent placer operations, tin streaming works, and gravity concentration plants.

    Why it matters · The sluice box is a small gravity plant in wood. Once set up, it rewards steady input rather than fiddly technique: more shoveling, less swirling. In Conduvia it should feel like a clear throughput upgrade that repays the investment in lumber, site prep, and water control.

    STsluice trough

    Channel segments that carry process water and slurry between stations.

    Plain wooden trough sections used to route water--and sometimes sediment--from sources to sluices, wheels, and settling boxes.

    Real-world use · Troughs marshal water to where it’s needed, maintain controlled slopes, and sometimes pre-wash or sort material as it travels.

    • When · Any era using water management for mills and washing operations.
    • Where · Mills, washing plants, tailings handling, and irrigation systems around processing sites.

    Why it matters · Controlling where water goes is as important as having water at all. Sluice troughs let crews re-use process water, split flows, and keep work areas from turning into unmanageable mud. In Conduvia they visually link water sources, sluices, wheels, and tailings areas into understandable circuits.

    SDspindle drill

    Straight-shaft drill twirled between the hands for small holes.

    A simple drill consisting of a sharpened shaft spun between the palms or with a simple bow or strap, used for light boring in soft materials.

    Real-world use · Spindle drills made holes for pegs, lashing points, and fittings in wood and bone before metal augers were common. They’re slow but require very little equipment.

    • When · Prehistoric through early historic periods, with variants persisting in folk crafts.
    • Where · Early woodworking, leatherwork, and household repair where only small holes are needed.

    Why it matters · The spindle drill shows up wherever “I just need a little hole” beats “let’s forge a new tool”. It’s inefficient compared to later brace-and-bit or twist drills, but dead simple to improvise from a straight stick and a flint or metal tip. In-game, it covers the very first boring tasks before dedicated drill rigs and powered machinery appear.

    SMstamp mill (manual trip hammer)

    A camshaft repeatedly lifts heavy hammers that drop onto ore, breaking it into smaller pieces and fines for washing.

    Real-world use · Early stampers range from hand-lifted hammers to cam-driven beams powered by waterwheels. The repeated blows reduce ore to sand-like grains, feeding sluices and dressing tables far more efficiently than hand crushing.

    • When · Stamp-like crushers appear in antiquity; water-driven stamp mills become common in European mining by the late medieval and early modern periods.
    • Where · On dressing floors below mine adits or beside streams where the same waterwheel can power both crushing and washing equipment.

    Why it matters · The manual trip hammer sits between a sledge and a full industrial stamp mill. It illustrates how a simple cam-and-follower system can automate heavy, boring labor. Because stamps consume a lot of power and create fines, they also motivate better tailings management and recovery--connecting mechanical design to resource efficiency.

    TBtailings box

    Catch box or basin placed at the end of a sluice or launder to trap tailings instead of letting them spread unchecked.

    Real-world use · Miners build wooden or masonry boxes at sluice outlets so sand and mud collect in a controlled place. Boxes can be cleaned out, moved, or later reprocessed without fouling downstream channels.

    • When · Common once sluicing and gravity separation become organized operations, especially around regulated or constrained sites.
    • Where · At the discharge of sluices, washing troughs, and other water-based concentrators.

    Why it matters · The tailings box is a small piece of infrastructure with big consequences. It keeps the site workable by preventing every wash from carving new channels and mudflats. It also quietly introduces environmental management and housekeeping as part of the flowsheet--things that historical operations often ignored until they caused trouble.

    T(tailpole (windmill yaw)

    Long lever used to swing a post mill body into the wind.

    A stout beam attached to the rear of a post mill body, used by workers or a simple cart to rotate the whole mill around its post to face the wind.

    Real-world use · Before automatic fantails, millers walked the tailpole around to adjust the mill’s orientation whenever the wind shifted, securing it with chains or brakes.

    • When · Middle Ages through early modern windmill practice, especially on early post mills.
    • Where · Post mills and some early smock mills lacking self-yawing mechanisms.

    Why it matters · The tailpole makes clear that early windmills weren’t “set and forget”. Millers constantly watched the weather and physically pushed the entire mill body around its post to stay on the wind. In Conduvia it reinforces that wind power starts as labor-intensive supervision, not free automated energy.

    TAtappet

    Block, arm, or lug that rides on a cam, converting the cam’s profile into timed lift-and-drop motion.

    Real-world use · In stamp mills and presses, tappets on a rotating shaft catch and lift stamp heads; as the cam turns past, the tappet slips free and the stamp falls. Different profiles and layouts tune the impact timing and height.

    • When · Wherever cam-driven machinery exists--from medieval water-powered hammers to later mechanical presses.
    • Where · On the shafts of stamp mills, fulling mills, and other early cam-driven machines that need rhythmic impacts.

    Why it matters · Tappets are the “shoes” that let a cam push on a heavy member without binding. They translate a smooth rotation into a lumpy sequence of events. In an educational sense they underline that much of pre-electric automation is geometry: clever shapes on a shaft dictating exactly when things lift, fall, or open.

    TAtar

    Thick, sticky liquid distilled from wood or resin during charcoal burning and pitch-making, used as a sealant, preservative, and fuel.

    Real-world use · Collected from kilns or dedicated tar pits, wood tar waterproofed hulls and rope, preserved wood, and fed into pitch, lamp fuel, and early chemical recipes.

    • When · Recorded in classical and medieval sources; widely used through the age of sail and into early industrial chemistry.
    • Where · Naval yards, ropewalks, timber works, and any charcoal operation that took care to condense and collect vapors.

    Why it matters · Where charcoal is made carefully, tar follows. As wood breaks down under heat, some of the heavy organic vapors can be cooled and condensed rather than simply burned away. In-game you can treat tar as the “dark glue” of pre-petroleum engineering--a material that bridges shipbuilding, waterproofing, and later chemistry. It ties charcoal-burning sites to shipyards, forges, and process plants.

    T(temper (sand or grog)

    Non-plastic grit--river sand, crushed stone, or grog--mixed into clay to tune its firing and working behavior.

    Real-world use · Makers adjust clay recipes by adding coarse particles. Sand improves drying and helps bricks keep shape; grog offers similar benefits with better thermal shock resistance for kiln furniture and crucibles.

    • When · A universal practice in pottery and brickmaking traditions world-wide.
    • Where · Anywhere clay is being tailored for bricks, pots, furnace linings, or tuyere blocks.

    Why it matters · Pure clay is too perfect: it shrinks, warps, and cracks. Temper deliberately makes the body “worse” in one sense so it can survive real-world use. For teaching, temper is a reminder that many historical materials are engineered composites. The player is not just digging “clay” but learning that careful recipes are needed for different tasks--tiles, bricks, cooking pots, furnace linings--each with their own temper mix.

    TRtreadle

    Foot pedal that converts leg motion into rotary or reciprocating power.

    A pedal rocked by the operator’s leg, linked by rod, strap, or crank to drive a shaft or tool while leaving the hands free.

    Real-world use · Treadles power lathes, sharpeners, bellows, sewing machines, and small pumps, letting a seated or standing operator work continuously.

    • When · Ancient examples in potters’ wheels and looms; widespread in medieval and early industrial craft machinery.
    • Where · Small shops and household-scale machinery where a single worker both powers and guides the tool.

    Why it matters · Human legs are stronger and more endurance-capable than arms. Treadles harness that advantage and free the hands to steer work, which is why so many early machines have foot pedals. In Conduvia they are the natural first upgrade from pure arm-driven tools toward continuous small-scale power.

    WLwater level

    Simple level using a water-filled tube to match heights.

    A length of clear tubing partly filled with water; the free surfaces at each end settle to the same gravitational level, allowing elevation to be transferred over distance.

    Real-world use · Builders use water levels to match the height of corners for foundations, sills, and dams even when they cannot see between points. It is cheap and surprisingly accurate over tens of meters.

    • When · Known in antiquity and common in traditional building into the modern era, especially where precision instruments are rare or expensive.
    • Where · Construction sites, canal and aqueduct works, terrace layouts, and anywhere large level planes are needed.

    Why it matters · A water level proves that fluids in connected vessels share the same height in gravity. That insight lets crews level long runs of foundations, flumes, and roads using nothing more exotic than a tube and patience. In Conduvia it explains how early settlements achieve surprisingly level floors and spillways without spirit levels or optics.

    W(waterwheel (overshot)

    Wheel fed from above so water weight turns it efficiently.

    A waterwheel where water enters at or near the top and fills buckets on the descending side, letting gravity on the water provide torque.

    Real-world use · Overshot wheels achieve high efficiency by using both the weight and some of the impulse of water, making them ideal where there is good head but limited flow.

    • When · Common from medieval Europe onward in hilly regions with streams suitable for leats and millraces.
    • Where · Mills sited on steep streams, ore stamping works, sawmills, and any installation designed around head rather than big rivers.

    Why it matters · Overshot wheels show how careful siting and civil works can turn modest streams into serious mechanical power. They reward investment in channels, reservoirs, and level control rather than simply dropping a wheel in the current. In Conduvia, building one should feel like a major infrastructure milestone that anchors a whole workshop district.

    W(waterwheel (undershot)

    Wheel driven by current pushing paddles at its base.

    A waterwheel placed in a flowing stream so that current pushes its lower paddles, relying mainly on water’s kinetic energy rather than head.

    Real-world use · Undershot wheels are easier to site--just span a river or channel--but less efficient than overshot designs, especially in shallow or slow water.

    • When · Some of the earliest European wheels were undershot; they remain common where terrain is flat and head is scarce.
    • Where · Lowland rivers, tidal mills, drainage works, and improvised installations in existing channels.

    Why it matters · Undershot wheels are the “quick and dirty” route to water power. They don’t need big head, but they waste much of the water’s potential, so they favor big flows and broad rivers. In Conduvia they give early access to water power while hinting that better siting and civil engineering will pay off later.

    WEwedge

    Tapered block driven in to split wood or ease stones apart.

    A triangular piece of wood or metal hammered into a joint or grain to multiply force and separate material.

    Real-world use · Wedges split logs, lift massive stones slightly for packing, and tension joints. Multiple wedges in sequence allow crews to open controlled cracks in rock or to free stuck machinery.

    • When · One of the classical simple machines; used since prehistory and present in every era of construction and quarrying.
    • Where · Wood yards, quarries, framing sites, and anywhere heavy pieces must be parted or pre-lifted.

    Why it matters · The wedge is a mechanical advantage device disguised as a chunk of material. By trading distance moved for force, it lets a moderate hammer blow create huge splitting pressure. In Conduvia it supports realistic timber splitting and stone handling long before powered breakers show up.

    WAwheel-and-axle

    Fundamental pair that turns torque at one radius into motion at another.

    A wheel rigidly fixed to a shaft so they rotate together, allowing force at the rim to move loads at the axle or vice versa.

    Real-world use · The wheel-and-axle appears in carts, winches, capstans, gears, and nearly all rotating machinery, often combined with ropes or chains for additional mechanical advantage.

    • When · Classical simple machine; widespread wherever wheeled transport or rotary machinery exists.
    • Where · Carts, hoists, mills, cranes, and every shafted machine.

    Why it matters · Wheel-and-axle is the basic template for most rotating machines. Understanding that the rim and shaft share rotation but not radius unlocks everything from winches to gearing. In Conduvia it sits at the conceptual root of nearly every transmission players build.

    WIwindlass

    Horizontal drum that winds rope or chain to lift or pull loads.

    A horizontal cylinder turned by cranks or bars, winding line to raise or lower buckets, cages, or loads in pits and over edges.

    Real-world use · Windlasses draw water from wells, haul ore from shafts, and tension lines. Ratchets or brakes keep the load from unwinding when the handle is released.

    • When · Ancient and medieval mining and well technology; common until superseded by powered hoists.
    • Where · Wells, mine heads, siege works, and any site that must raise loads vertically with simple gear.

    Why it matters · The windlass is a simple, intuitive hoist: one turn equals one drum circumference of line. Adding pawls and ratchets makes it safer, and pairing with blocks multiplies its pull. In Conduvia it’s a natural upgrade over hand-over-hand hauling for wells and shallow shafts.

    WDwooden dowel

    Cylindrical wooden pin for blind joints and peg gears.

    A round wooden pin, usually made from straight-grained stock, fitted into bored holes to reinforce joints or act as pegs in low-speed gears and couplings.

    Real-world use · Dowels pin together boards, reinforce mortise-and-tenon joints, and serve as removable pivots and pegs in wooden gears or linkages.

    • When · Used throughout historical woodworking; especially common in furniture and timber framing before metal fasteners were cheap.
    • Where · Joinery and furniture shops, peg gear trains, tool jigs, and modular wooden rigs.

    Why it matters · Dowels extend the idea of “wooden peg” into true blind fastening. By drilling and pinning below the surface, builders keep faces clean while still locking parts together. In Conduvia they justify compact wooden mechanisms and gear trains that don’t bristle with visible lashings.

    WGwooden gear (peg-toothed)

    Wooden gear rimmed with replaceable peg teeth for slow drives.

    A wooden wheel whose rim carries individual hardwood pegs as teeth, meshing with a matching gear to transmit motion at low to moderate speed.

    Real-world use · Millwrights used peg gears in windmills and watermills to step down or redirect power. When a tooth broke, the peg could be replaced without rebuilding the whole wheel.

    • When · Medieval through early industrial milling and pumping equipment; still seen in restored mills.
    • Where · Windmills, watermills, early factories driven by water or wind power, and demonstration machinery.

    Why it matters · Peg gears show how you can build real gear trains out of purely wooden technology. By making each tooth a separate peg, wear and breakage become maintenance problems instead of disasters. In Conduvia they anchor believable, repairable gearing between waterwheels, windmills, and line shafts.

    WPwooden peg

    Tapered peg driven through holes to lock wooden joints.

    A small, slightly tapered stick or pin cut from tough wood, driven through aligned holes in timbers or planks to fasten them mechanically.

    Real-world use · Pegs secure mortise-and-tenon joints, pin rails to posts, and attach iron fittings without nails. They can be split and wedged to lock permanently or left removable.

    • When · Staple of timber framing and shipbuilding from antiquity through early industrial construction.
    • Where · Framing yards, barns, scaffolding, shipyards, and any work favoring all-wood construction.

    Why it matters · Wooden pegs are the wooden equivalent of rivets and bolts. They are easy to make on site, don’t rust, and can grip surprisingly well when driven into slightly offset holes (“draw-bored” joints). In-game they enable strong timber frames even before iron nails and bolts are practical.
