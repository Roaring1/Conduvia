### From Steam Ironworks to Steel & Power

Your steam plant has proven the principle: controlled heat, moving pressure, turning shafts.

Tier 3 asks you to scale all of it.

- **Blast furnace** replaces your bloomery and wrought-iron forge with coke-fired pig iron production
- **Bessemer and open-hearth converters** turn pig iron into *steel* -- harder, tougher, weldable
- **Rolled products** become structural material for bridges, ships, and boiler shells
- **High-pressure boilers** push your engines far beyond Tier 2 limits
- **Compound engines** do more work with less coal
- **Dynamos** are steam turned to electricity
- **DC networks** wire your site for the first time
- **Motors** put power exactly where you need it, not just where a shaft happens to run

This is the tier where your works stop being a collection of machines and start being a site.

### Coke blast furnace

Your bloomery reduced iron without melting it. This is different.

A coke blast furnace runs hot enough to produce *liquid* pig iron --
iron with 3–4% dissolved carbon, poured from a tap hole at the base.
It's not wrought iron. It's brittle cast metal that feeds the converters.

The hot-blast stove preheats the incoming air, cutting coke consumption
by 30–40% compared to cold-blast operation. It's one of the most
important efficiency upgrades in 19th-century metallurgy.

Load ore, coke, and limestone flux in alternating layers from the top.
Tap pig iron from the bottom. The furnace runs continuously -- never cools.



<div class="qb-hint" markdown>
**Tip** · **Plan ahead: this chapter needs ~200+ pig iron total.**
This quest proves the furnace works (40 pig iron).
The Bessemer quest that follows consumes 80, and the open-hearth quest consumes another 80.
Keep your blast furnace running between quests and build a stockpile before you start converting.
</div>

### Bessemer converter: fast bulk steel

Pig iron is brittle because it contains too much carbon.
The Bessemer process fixes this in minutes, not hours.

Load the converter with molten pig iron.
Tilt it upright. Blast cold air through the tuyeres at the base.
The air burns out excess carbon -- the heat is generated by the carbon combustion,
not from external fuel. The heat is self-sustaining.

In 15–20 minutes: bulk steel.

Bessemer is fast and cheap. It's not precise -- composition control is poor.
Use it for rails and structural shapes where exact carbon content matters less.



<div class="qb-hint" markdown>
**Tip** · **Pig iron demand for this chapter is high.** This quest alone consumes 80 pig iron, and the open-hearth furnace quest that follows consumes another 80.  Before you start, make sure your blast furnace can keep up -- tap and stockpile at least 200 pig iron before you begin converting.  Falling short mid-quest is frustrating.

### Siemens–Martin open-hearth furnace

Bessemer is fast. Open-hearth is careful.

The furnace uses *regenerators* -- alternating chambers of heated brickwork
that pre-heat combustion air by recycling exhaust heat. This lets the furnace
reach temperatures high enough to fully melt the charge rather than just blowing
air through it.

Full melting means you can add scrap, control the slag chemistry, and sample
the heat repeatedly before tapping. The result: better composition control
and higher-quality steel for pressure vessels and load-bearing structures.

Slower than Bessemer, but the furnace that built bridges, battleships, and boilers.
</div>

### Rails, beams, and boiler plate

Steel is only useful when it can be shaped into precise structural forms.
A rolling mill squeezes hot steel between shaped rollers to produce uniform
cross-sections: rails, I-beams, angles, flat plate.

Rolling replaces forging for bulk production. The shapes are consistent.
The tolerances are tight enough to bolt together.

**What this unlocks:**
- Steel rails: your transport network scales with them
- Steel beams: buildings and bridges that don't rely on timber or cast iron
- Boiler plate: steel pressure vessels for the next stage of engine upgrades
- Gusset plates: structural joints that actually hold under dynamic load

The rolling mill is one of the loudest, most impressive machines in the works.
It should look like it.
</div>

### Steel boilers: higher pressure

Your Tier 2 boilers ran on wrought iron plate at moderate pressure.
That's a hard ceiling -- wrought iron fatigues at high pressure cycles.

Steel plate is stronger, more consistent, and can be rolled thicker.
Riveted steel shells with proper stays can safely run at 2–3× the pressure
your old boilers could handle.

Higher pressure means the same mass of steam does more work per stroke.
Your engines are about to get significantly more powerful for the same fuel.

The weighted safety valve isn't cosmetic. Overpressure kills people and destroys sites.

### Compound engines: do more with the same steam

A simple engine uses steam once, then exhausts it at near-boiler pressure --
wasting most of its energy.

A compound engine uses steam in stages: a high-pressure cylinder takes the initial
expansion, then exhausts into a larger low-pressure cylinder for a second expansion.
Same boiler. Same coal. Significantly more shaft power.

The Corliss valve gear adds variable cut-off: steam admission stops partway
through the stroke, letting expansion do more work before exhaust.
This is the engine that made 19th-century mills competitive.

The flyball governor keeps shaft speed stable under varying load -- important
for workshops, rolling mills, and eventually dynamos.
</div>

### Steam-driven dynamo house

Your engines turn shafts. A dynamo turns a shaft into direct current.

The principle: coils of wire rotating in a magnetic field induce voltage.
Early dynamos use wrought iron cores for the field magnets
and laminated armature cores to reduce eddy current losses.

Belt-couple a reliable engine to two dynamos in a dedicated bay.
Wire the outputs to copper bus bars at the switchboard.
The machine shop now has electrical capability.

This is the moment your site produces electricity.
Everything that follows -- lights, motors, instruments -- is downstream of this.
</div>

### Low-voltage DC network and lights

Electricity is only useful when it goes somewhere.

Run insulated copper wire from your dynamo switchboard to lamp fixtures
across the works. The circuit is simple: dynamo → switchboard → fuse → switch → lamp.
The voltage drop over distance limits how far you can push a DC network --
this is not a long-distance transmission system, it's a local lighting and small-power grid.

The incandescent lamps use carbon filaments (1879-era technology).
They're fragile, short-lived, and dim by later standards.
But they are *electric light*. Your site can now operate after dark.

The wooden switchboard, knife switches, and rewireable fuses are early electrical
safety discipline. They are not decorative.
</div>

### Motors on the shop floor

A DC motor is a dynamo run in reverse: send current through it and the shaft turns.

The first practical DC motors were installed in factories in the 1880s.
They did not immediately replace line shafting -- the transition was decades-long
and gradual. But they changed what was *possible*:

- A machine tool could now run independently of the main shaft
- A hoist could be placed exactly where lifting was needed
- A department could be stopped for maintenance without halting the whole plant

Install DC motors on at least four key tools or hoists.
Keep some shafting -- the transition is real, not instant.
The remaining shafting will disappear in Tier 4 and beyond.
</div>
