    Iron & steel — the backbone chain¶

    Iron is the metal everything else leans on. Plates and beams alone are the key to dozens of machines, rails, and buildings, so this is the chain you'll come back to over and over. In Conduvia you take iron the whole way: from a soft lump pulled out of a muddy swamp and baked in a clay oven, all the way up to giant modern furnaces that melt old scrap back into fresh steel. Every step here is how it's really done.

    Two eras, two primary routes

    Bloomery → wrought iron (Tier 1): solid-state reduction in a clay furnace; the iron never melts. Sponge bloom is forged out to wrought iron. Your earliest, charcoal-only iron. Blast furnace → pig iron → steel (Tier 2–4): coke + flux melt the ore to high-carbon pig iron, then a converter burns the carbon back down to steel. The modern mass-production spine — refined three honest ways: Bessemer, Basic Oxygen (BOF), and Electric Arc (EAF).

    Getting the rock furnace-ready¶

    You can't just shovel raw crushed rock into a furnace. The dust clogs it up, and the worthless bits waste fuel. So first you clean the ore, then you clump it back into chunks the furnace likes. There are two kinds of iron rock, and each gets cleaned a different way:

    • Magnetite is naturally magnetic. So you run the crushed rock past magnets and they pull out a clean, iron-rich powder, leaving the junk behind. (The fancy name for this is magnetic separation.)
    • Hematite is barely magnetic, so the magnets can't grab it yet. You roast it in a little smoke first, which makes it magnetic, then run it past the magnets.

    That clean powder is too fine and dusty to feed straight in, so you clump it into chunks one of two ways:

    • Sinter — bake the powder with a little fuel and crushed limestone until the bits fuse into a hard, crumbly cake. The baking burns off sulfur as a gas, which you pipe over to the acid factory.
    • Pellets — roll the damp powder with a pinch of clay into little balls (think meatballs), then bake them rock-hard.

    Ironmaking — three ways to win the metal¶

    Route What happens Product
    Bloomery (T1) Solid-state reduction in a clay furnace; bloom never melts, forged to bar wrought iron (soft, tough, low-C)
    Blast furnace (T2) Coke → CO reduces oxide as it descends (Fe₂O₃/Fe₃O₄ + CO → Fe + CO₂); limestone fluxes gangue to slag pig iron (~4 % C, brittle)
    Direct reduction (DRI) (T3) Coal/CO reduce pellets below melting point sponge iron (porous, metallic — clean EAF charge)

    The tall blast furnace likes those sinter chunks better than raw rock — air flows through them more easily. And the new direct-reduction shaft turns the baked pellets into spongy iron without ever fully melting them.

    Steelmaking — burning the carbon back out¶

    Fresh-from-the-furnace iron (pig iron) has way too much carbon mixed in, which makes it brittle, like a dry cracker. To turn it into steel, you blow air or oxygen through the hot metal to burn most of that carbon back out. Conduvia models the three ways that actually matter:

    Furnace · station Charge How Tier
    Bessemer (existing) pig iron air blast burns out C (historical) T2
    Open hearth (existing) pig iron + coal slow, controllable bath T3
    Basic Oxygen (BOF / LD) pig iron + ~25 % scrap + burnt lime pure-O₂ blow, autogenous heat, basic slag pulls P & S T3
    Electric Arc (EAF) sponge iron + scrap + lime electric arc melts a cold charge — the recycling route T4

    The BOF (basic oxygen furnace) makes most of the world's brand-new steel. The EAF (electric arc furnace) is the recycling one — it melts down old scrap metal with a giant electric spark. Both are in the game, and both turn steel scrap into a real ingredient you can gather out in the world: old rails, busted machines, and demolition junk, exactly like real recyclers do.

    Stainless — keeping the chromium¶

    Stainless steel is just steel with chromium mixed in — that's the part that stops it from rusting. But there's a catch: if you blow oxygen through the hot metal to burn out the carbon, you burn away the pricey chromium too. The fix is a machine called the AOD converter. It mixes the oxygen with a do-nothing gas (argon) so the carbon burns off while the chromium stays put. Real factories make about three-quarters of all stainless steel this exact way.

    2 steel + ferrochrome + ferronickel + burnt lime → 2 stainless + slag

    Ferroalloys have their own chains

    The AOD step consumes ferrochrome and ferronickel. Both now have honest, furnace-deep upstreams — proper chromite→ferrochrome and laterite/sulfide→ferronickel chains — documented in the Ferroalloys field guide.

    Byproduct & cross-chain loops¶

    • Slag — every time you melt metal, a layer of glassy junk floats on top. That's slag. Don't toss it; it's handy as building filler.
    • Tailings — the leftover worthless rock from cleaning the ore.
    • Sulfur gas off the sinter line feeds the sulfuric-acid factory.
    • Roasted pyrite leftovers from the acid chain are basically ready-made iron ore — drop them straight back into ironmaking.
    • Sponge iron melts down into nearly pure iron, great for electromagnet cores and clean mixing stock.

    The chain at a glance¶

    flowchart TD
  MAG["crushed magnetite"] -->|LIMS| CONC["iron concentrate"]
  HEM["crushed hematite"] -->|mag-roast + sep| CONC
  CONC -->|sinter strand| SINT["sinter"]
  CONC -->|pelletizer| PEL["pellets"]
  SINT -->|blast furnace| PIG["pig iron"]
  PEL -->|DRI shaft| DRI["sponge iron"]
  PIG -->|BOF + scrap + lime| STEEL["steel"]
  DRI -->|EAF + scrap + lime| STEEL
  SCRAP["salvaged scrap"] --> STEEL
  STEEL -->|hot rolling| PLATE["plate / beam / rail"]
  STEEL -->|AOD + ferroalloys| SS["stainless"]
  DRI -->|remelt| PURE["pure iron"]
  SINT -.SO2.-> ACID["→ sulfuric acid"]

    Tier ladder

    Bloomery (T1) → blast furnace + Bessemer + beneficiation/sinter (T2) → BOF + pellets + DRI + open hearth (T3) → EAF + AOD stainless (T4). Hot rolling rides on top to turn ingots into plate, beam, and rail — the parts the rest of the tree actually consumes.
